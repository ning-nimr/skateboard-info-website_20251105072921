The World of Skateboarding

What is Skateboarding?

Skateboarding is both an action sport and a form of recreation in which a rider balances on and maneuvers a small board mounted on wheels. Over time, it has evolved into a rich subculture that spans sport, art, and lifestyle.

Historical Evolution

Early 20th Century: Surfers attached roller-skate wheels to wooden planks to "surf" on concrete when waves were flat.
1957-1959: First commercially manufactured skateboards appeared, produced by companies like Chicago Roller Skate and Roller Derby.
1969: Larry Stevenson patented the kicktail, enabling riders to pop the board off the ground for tricks.
Early 1970s: Frank Nasworthy introduced polyurethane wheels, significantly improving grip and speed.
1978: Alan "Ollie" Gelfand pioneered the ollie, a foundational hands-free jump technique.

Modern Skateboard Design

A typical skateboard deck today is made of 7–8 layers of maple plywood, fitted with trucks (axles), cushions for steering, and polyurethane wheels. Modern boards are designed longer and narrower, with concave shaping for better control and trick execution.

Culture and Influence

Rooted in surfer culture of Southern California, skateboarding evolved into a vibrant subculture characterized by individualism, fashion, DIY ethos, and artistic expression—including graphics, music, skate videos, and zines.

Skateboarding has developed into a professional sport with events like the X Games (since 1995) and Olympic inclusion starting at Tokyo 2020, featuring disciplines like street and park skating.
